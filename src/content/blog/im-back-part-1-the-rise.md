Hey. It's been a minute.

By "a minute" I mean the better part of two years. By "hey" I mean *holy shit, where do I even begin*.

The last real post here was September 2023. I was a fresh immigrant in Lahti, jetlagged and confused, wondering where all the people were on a Sunday morning. And a little before that, in another rant, I signed off on a post by basically going: *fuck formal cybersecurity, I'm going to chase what's fun and worry about staying relevant later.* Past me, if you somehow scrolled forward to read this: you had no idea what you were signing up for. It worked out. It also nearly killed me. Both of those are true, and I'm going to tell you the whole thing.

It's too much for one post though, so we're doing it in two. This one is the good part. The part where the line goes up and to the right and I feel like I've got it figured out. Enjoy it while it lasts, because part two is where the floor gives way.

## I accidentally became a community person

The whole thing started as a favor, honestly. Back in 2024 there was a hackathon with the City of Lahti called **Synergy**, and my friend Jazen basically dragged me into it as a fun weekend project. I showed up thinking I was there to build something cool and go home. What I did not realize is that I'd just quietly signed myself up to run corporate relations for an entire entrepreneurship society.

See, Jazen had clocked something about me before I had: that I'm weirdly good with people and companies. So he pulled me onto his team, and somewhere between "come do this cool hackathon" and "wait, am I on a *board* now," I'd become **LahtiES**' Corporate Relations lead. Board seat and everything. Which is a fancy way of saying I was now responsible for making people who weren't in the room care about us.

(Remember Jazen. He drags me into the best rooms of my life more than once in this story, and one of those times is the reason there's a part two of me around to write this at all.)

When I started, "us" was about 5 people. By the end of my term we were **250+**. I would love to take full credit but that would be a lie, so: shoutout to Chaska and Maggie, genuinely, that growth is as much yours as anyone's. What I *did* manage to do was drag our name into rooms it had no business being in yet. Business Finland. Aalto. Other orgs that, a year prior, I couldn't have gotten a meeting with if I'd shown up in person with a cake.

I'm not going to pretend this came for free. It came at the direct expense of my actual studies, which I quietly let fall off a cliff. And look, I know how that sounds. But I'll be honest with you the way I'm honest with myself: a lot of what we were being taught, I already knew. Sitting in a lecture re-learning something I could teach felt like a worse use of my one finite life than actually building the thing the lecture was theoretically preparing me for. Fight me. Or don't. My grades already did.

## The startup that taught me what not to be

While all this was happening, an opportunity landed in my lap: B2B sales for a startup I'm going to call `[ REDACTED ]`, because the more I sit with this the less I want to hand them free SEO.

The pitch was genuinely cool. Mycelium. Mushroom-based materials, thinking way past packaging. One of the ideas was sending the stuff up for lunar regolith structures, actual build-on-the-moon energy. My best friend was already involved, and I trusted her being there, so I jumped in. The opportunity was a great place to learn, and boy did I learn. Just, uh, not the lessons on the brochure.

Here's what B2B sales at `[ REDACTED ]` actually looked like, from where I sat:

- We got led in circles. Endless motion, no destination.
- Students got used as free interns. Nothing on paper except NDAs, which tells you exactly which direction the founder was worried about value flowing.
- The personal and the professional got blended into a slurry. Meetings turned into trauma-dumps. Boundaries, what boundaries.
- And the killer: there was almost no actual chemical or materials-science knowledge behind the thing. Which makes selling it *impossible*. We had a regional waste-management company dangling a potential **250k** contract, and there was simply no way to close it, because closing it required knowing what we were actually selling.

Oh, and the founder started showing up to meetings reeking of alcohol.

We stuck it out for about a week of that before my friend and I looked at each other and quit. I want to be fair: this is my experience, one person's view from one seat. But it's the truth of what I saw, and the lesson stuck harder than any course I skipped. I know *exactly* what kind of founder I refuse to become now. That's not nothing. That's most of the map, honestly.

## The part where I feel invincible

So I did the obvious thing: I doubled down on LahtiES. Poured the energy back into outreach, into the network, into being useful to people who were actually building. And it *worked*. My personal network went from "some classmates" to a genuine web of people I'm still proud to know. I met founders, operators, investors, people who were playing a game I badly wanted to be good at.

For the first time since I'd landed in this quiet, empty, weirdly wonderful country, things felt *stable*. I had a role. I had a network. I had a reputation that was starting to precede me in a good way. I remember a stretch in early-to-mid 2025 where I genuinely thought: *okay. I've cracked it. From here it's just execution.*

Reader, I had not cracked it.

Everything I just described is real and I'm proud of it and it mattered. It's also the exact setup a story uses right before it takes everything away from you to see what you're actually made of. Summer was coming, and summer 2025 had plans for me that I could not possibly have seen coming.

That's part two. It's a harder one to write, and probably a harder one to read. But it's the part that actually made me who I am now, so it's the one that matters most.

More soon. Sooner than two years this time, I promise.

~ A.
