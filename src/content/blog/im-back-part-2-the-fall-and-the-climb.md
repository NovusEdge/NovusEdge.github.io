> ⚠️ **Heads up before you start:** this one gets into being flat broke, a bad depressive spiral, and suicidal thinking. If that's a landmine for you right now, skip it, genuinely no hard feelings. And if you're sitting in that exact hole as you read this: please talk to an actual person, not just the void. I'm really glad I did.

Part one ended with me feeling invincible. Network, reputation, a role, momentum. I told you to enjoy it while it lasted.

Here's the floor giving way.

## Summer 2025 took everything

I'm not going to get into the details, but that summer there was conflict back home between India and Pakistan, and when the dust settled I had lost basically all of my assets. Not "tighten the belt" broke. *Flat* broke. Zero, and the wrong side of zero.

The timing could not have been more surgical. Tuition was due in about two months. Somewhere around **9k** for that, plus rent, plus the small inconvenience of needing to eat. And here's the part that turns a bad situation into a vice: I'm an immigrant. If I didn't pay, I didn't just fail a semester. I got removed from the country. The whole life I'd built in part one, the network, the board seat, all of it, evaporates the moment I can't produce a number I do not have.

So I did the sane thing first. I looked for work. Any work.

And I mean *any*. I sent out over **600 job applications**. I stopped waiting for replies and started walking in the door: restaurants, Burger King, other fast food places, construction sites, factories. I would have washed dishes, hauled bricds, anything with a paycheck attached. Nobody took me. Six hundred applications and a dozen in-person asks and the answer was a uniform, echoing *no*.

## The bottom

I want to be honest here, because part one was easy to write and this part is not, and I said I'd tell you the whole thing.

I spiraled. The rejections stacked up and something in me came apart. I'd swing from a burst of hope, *this one, this one will land*, straight back down into a flat, gray certainty that it was over. Days blurred. I got suicidal. Not in the abstract, poetic way you write about later. In the way where I walked around the ski jump tower here in Lahti and stood there and pictured myself going over the top of it.

I was too afraid to do it. I'm writing this, so, you already know that. But I want that sentence to sit there plainly instead of getting dressed up, because if you've been at the top of your own version of that tower, I need you to know someone who builds a life you might envy from the outside was standing there too.

When jumping wasn't it, my broken brain found a different exit. I thought: *okay, if I'm going out, it may as well mean something.* Ukraine was taking anyone and everyone. I know how drones work. I could be an engineer out there. So I actually did it, I found a recruiter, and I got drafted into the 4th International Legion.

I had about **700 dollars** to my name. That was, almost to the euro, enough to buy some gear, a drone kit, a ticket to Poland, and a bus ticket to the Ukrainian border. I did the math. It fit. That's the detail that still unsettles me, that it *fit*.

I started calling my friends to say goodbye.

## The people who wouldn't let me stay down

One of those calls was to my best friend. She was the reason part one had a best friend in it, and she was the reason part two didn't end where it was heading. She talked to me. Really talked to me. And then she spotted me some cash to keep me standing while I figured out literally anything else.

Around the same time, a genuinely absurd and genuinely beautiful thing happened. My board at **LahtiES** caught wind that I was drowning, and we convened an actual, full board meeting about it. And one of the *serious* options we mapped out, whiteboard and all, was that I marry one of our board members, on paper, to keep my residency. We'd have been a gay couple, purely bureaucratically, and honestly I've never felt more held by a group of people than watching them earnestly plot immigration fraud to keep me in the country. We mapped the whole route. I love them for that and I'll never stop telling this story.

I had a shot at Forward by LUTES around then too, and I had to let it go, because the person who would have shown up for it was not in any state to. That one still stings a little. But surviving came first.

Then my uncle in America stepped in and helped me get started with freelancing. He's a good person. He is also, and I say this with love, a genuinely miserable human to work *with*. Both things, at once, kept me fed. I scraped together a loan from a family friend for the tuition, and freelancing kept the lights on week to week.

## The burning thing

Here's the part I still don't fully understand about myself.

I was still suicidal. That didn't switch off because a friend caught me. And *at the same time*, underneath it, there was this absolutely rabid urge to make something. Something big. Something remarkable, exceptional, undeniable. Like the only way I could justify still being here was to build something that earned the space I was taking up.

So I did the only thing that ever makes sense to me. I worked. Roughly **18 hours a day**, stopping basically only to sleep, sometimes sleeping in the office. That office was **CrazyTown Lahti**, and Toni and Sini over there, thank you, you have no idea what it meant to have somewhere to be and people who were glad you were there.

I was building a thing called `queryables`. I'm not going to explain it here, it earns its own post, but the short version is I thought it was good enough to raise capital around and turn into a real venture, and I was going to bet everything I had left, which was mostly just hours, on being right.

## The climb

Turns out I was right, just not in the shape I expected.

Jazen Cordero, our ex-Chair at LahtiES and my friend, heard what I was building. He was interning at **Antler Helsinki** at the time, and he introduced me around. Antler had a portfolio company, **BotSpot**, building in the *exact* same domain I was. So I talked to them. They liked me, and they liked what I'd made enough that they didn't want to compete with it. They wanted to buy it outright and bring me on as their **Founding Engineer**.

I said yes.

That was September 2025. For the first time since the summer, the ground under me was solid. I volunteered for **SLUSH** around then, back in rooms full of people building things, except this time I wasn't performing stability, I actually had it. The line started pointing up again, and this time it wasn't a setup for a fall. It was just... up.

## What the pit taught me

There's a lot more after this. There's a whole arc I haven't touched, the AI stuff, the memory stuff, the company I'm building now. That's for other posts. This one had a specific job.

The job was to say, out loud and on my own domain where I can't take it back: I was as far down as a person gets, close enough to the edge to do the arithmetic on a one-way bus ticket, and I'm still here, and I'm building. Not because I'm strong. Because a handful of people, a best friend, a board that plotted a fake marriage for me, an uncle, a family friend, Toni and Sini, Jazen, refused to let me disappear quietly.

If you take one thing from the two years I vanished, take that. The urge to create is what pulled me forward, but it was *people* who kept me alive long enough to follow it. Don't try to do the pit alone. I almost did, and almost is the whole story.

And if you're in it right now, actually in it, reading this from your own version of that tower: it does not stay this dark. I know that's the most worn-out sentence on the internet, and I know it lands like nothing when you're down there, because it landed like nothing on me too. But I'm the proof it's true. Less than a year ago the math on a one-way ticket fit in my budget. Today I'm building things I'd have given anything to be near. The distance between those two points is so much shorter than it feels from the bottom. There is always more hope left than the pit lets you count. Stay long enough to be surprised by it. Please.

Thanks for reading this far. Reach out, genuinely. It's better with company. It always was.

~ A.
