The masthead on my stack page says **"I BUILD COOL SHIT."** in big fat letters. I stand by it. It is also, on most Tuesdays, a bit of a lie.

Here's the truth nobody puts on their landing page: a huge chunk of my week is not spent building cool shit. It's spent making a slide about the cool shit. Then on a call about the slide. Then updating a spreadsheet that projects how much cool shit we can afford to build next quarter, assuming the call goes well, which it might not. The building is the thing I love and it is somehow the smallest slice of the pie.

Nobody warns you about this part. Or, ok, people *warn* you, in the way people warn you that university is expensive or that Finland is cold. You nod, you think "sure, makes sense," and then you actually live it and realize the warning did not remotely prepare you for the texture of the thing.

## Founder and CTO are two different jobs and they hate each other

When you're the CTO, your instinct is: there's a problem, go open the file, fix the problem. That instinct is *good*. It's the whole reason I'm here. The founder half of me, though, spends its day being told that the highest-leverage thing I can do is *not* open the file. It's to be in the room. It's to tell a coherent story about a future that doesn't exist yet to someone who is deciding whether to bet money on it existing.

And those two people, the one who wants to be in the code and the one who has to be in the room, are constantly fighting over my calendar. Some days the CTO wins and I ship something and feel like myself. Some days the founder wins and I close a conversation that keeps the lights on for three more months. Both matter. Neither feels like enough on its own. The trick, apparently, is being ok with doing each one at maybe 70%, because 100% of one means 0% of the other and the company needs both.

I have not fully made peace with this. I'm being honest with you.

## Fundraising is a skill and I was not born with it

Raising investment is its own craft, and it is *weird*. You are simultaneously the most optimistic person in the room (because you have to be, because if you don't believe it nobody will) and the person who knows exactly how held-together-with-duct-tape the present actually is. You're selling a future while standing in a present that would give the buyer a heart attack if they saw the repo at 3am.

That's not lying. I want to be clear about that, because early on it *felt* like lying and it messed with my head. It's not. It's that a startup is a bet on a trajectory, and your job is to make the trajectory legible: here's where we are, here's the wedge, here's why the line goes up and why *we're* the ones who draw it. The duct tape is fine. Everyone's present is duct tape. The people who win are the ones who can hold "this is barely working" and "this is going to be enormous" in their head at the same time without going insane.

I'm still learning to hold both. Some weeks I hold neither.

## So why do it

Because of a thing I wrote back in 2023, actually. In an old post I said we're all in an age where it's either *create* or *be consumed*. I meant it as a vibe back then, a nice line to end a rant on. Turns out it's just... true, and living it is a lot less poetic than saying it.

Building a company is the most concentrated version of "create" I've found. It's the thing where, if I don't do it, it doesn't get done, and the shape of the thing is entirely my problem. That's terrifying and it's also the most alive I've felt. The spreadsheets and the decks and the calls are the tax you pay for getting to point the whole apparatus at a problem *you* chose and say "no, we're doing it like this."

Would I love to just be in the code all day building cool shit? Obviously. Hell yes. But the code doesn't get to exist at all unless somebody does the unglamorous half, and right now that somebody is also me.

I don't have this figured out. I want to say that plainly, because there's enough founder content out there that pretends the author cracked some code and is now dispensing wisdom from a mountain. I'm in it. It's messy. Ask me in a year.

If you're doing the same thing, the founder-and-builder-at-once thing, and it's kicking your ass some weeks, reach out. Genuinely. It's a lonely little corner and it's better with company.
